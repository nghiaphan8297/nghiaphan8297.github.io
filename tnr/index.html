A Brief History

Times New Roman is named after the Times of London, British Newspaper. The font is drawn by Victor Lardent, an artist in the Times, with Stanley Morison from Monotype leading the project. The font is completed in 1931 after some issue between Monotype and Linotype and then commonly be used in newspaper. Being used as the font for a daily medium, the font slowly is familiarlized by millions of people. More and more documents is printed after the font. it even be liscensed by Apple for Macintosh in 1984 and by Microsoft for Windoms in 1992, which make the number of documents set in Times New Roman exploded. Origin

The origin of the font is abit of a mystery. The 16th-centyry French typographer Robert Granjon has been said to be a starting point of Times New Roman. While some other Monotype font could be the direct ancestor of it. For example, Plantin, side by side with Time New Roman, has some unmistakable similarity. See the article

Criticism

Despite its success, Times New Roman recieved many criticisms. There's not much to complain about Times New Roman, as it is a general font that is created for newspaper. But when Times New Roman is used as decoratives on a book, advertisement, etc, it says, "I submitted to the font of least resistance." As Matthew Butterick criticize the font: "Times New Roman is not a font choice so much as the absence of a font choice, like the blackness of deep space is not a color. To look at Times New Roman is to gaze into the void." See the article
